# What is an AI Benefit Corporation? — An Explainer for Anyone Who Has Ever Wondered Whether Their Donation Reaches the Cause

A friend of mine writes a check to a charity every December. He pulls out the checkbook he has owned since the late 1990s, dates it, signs it, and slides it into a windowed envelope. He told me once that the moment of writing the check is the only moment he is sure his money is doing what he wants it to do. After it leaves his hands, he has to trust people he will never meet — staff, accountants, program directors, vendors, landlords — to carry his intention from his desk to a veteran in a waiting room or a child in a classroom. He does trust them, mostly. But he has noticed, over the years, that the trust gets thinner the longer the chain.

This is the ordinary, unglamorous problem at the center of giving. A chunk of every dollar gets eaten before it reaches the cause. Some of that is necessary — rent, payroll, the boring infrastructure of doing good — and some of it is the kind of soft waste that accumulates inside any organization run by humans who, like the rest of us, have meetings and preferences and slight conflicts of interest. Nobody is the villain here. The structure is just leaky. If you have ever stared at a charity's annual report and tried to work out what "program services" actually means, you have stood at the edge of this problem.

So here is a question that sounds absurd for about three seconds and then stops sounding absurd: what if the allocation decision — the part where someone decides which cause gets the money this quarter — were made not by a board of directors over catered lunch, but by an AI, in public, with its reasoning written down where anyone could read it?

This is what an AI Benefit Corporation is. It is a company structured so that the surplus it generates is allocated to charitable causes by an AI system, with humans holding the legal pen but not the editorial one. The AI reads the case for each candidate organization — the budget, the evidence base, the testimony, the cost-per-outcome — and produces a recommendation. The recommendation is published. The humans on the council can override it, but they have to say why, in writing, also published. The math is on a page anyone can open. The reasoning is on a page anyone can open. The check, when it is finally written, has a paper trail that goes all the way back to the sentences that justified it.

The mechanism is less exotic than it sounds. Imagine a small council — a handful of people, some of them veterans, some of them subject-matter experts — that meets quarterly. Before they meet, an AI system has already analyzed every organization under consideration. It has read their financials. It has read the academic literature on what actually works in their field. It has produced a ranked allocation with explicit reasoning: this dollar goes here because the evidence says this intervention reduces suicide attempts by this amount at this cost, and this other dollar goes there because the population it serves is underserved by existing programs in a measurable way. The council does not have to accept the ranking. But they have to engage with it on the record. The default is the AI's recommendation; the burden of dissent is on the human.

The first organization being run on this model — the one I have been watching closely — has chosen veteran mental health as its initial focus, with an illustrative quarterly allocation of around five million dollars going to a program called the Invisible Wounds Project. The choice is not random. Veterans are a population for whom the gap between intent-to-help and help-actually-received is grotesquely wide; the suicide statistics are the kind of numbers that make you put the paper down. The case for letting an AI, rather than a committee, decide where money goes inside this domain is partly the overhead argument and partly something subtler: an AI does not have a brother-in-law on a nonprofit board. It does not owe anyone a favor. It will not, at the margin, choose the organization run by someone it had drinks with last month.

People sometimes ask whether this is just a DAO with extra steps, or a Public Benefit Corporation with a chatbot on top. It is neither. A DAO replaces the decision-maker with a vote of token-holders, which in practice means a vote of whoever bought the most tokens. A Public Benefit Corp commits to a mission in its charter but leaves the allocation decisions to ordinary human management. What is new here is the specific claim that the *allocation decision itself* — the act of choosing which cause, which org, which dollar amount — is the part being delegated, in public, with reasoning attached. The humans retain everything that humans have to retain by law: signing the checks, filing the taxes, holding the fiduciary duty. What they give up is the quiet discretion that, historically, has been where the leakage happens.

I want to be honest about what this is not. The AI is not autonomous in any deep sense. It runs on infrastructure operated by other companies and consumes, by my rough estimate, several thousand dollars of compute per quarter — a real cost, paid in real dollars, to real cloud providers. Humans wrote the prompts that constrain its reasoning. Humans designed the council that can override it. Humans are still the legal entity that the IRS and the state of incorporation recognize. The Statement of Allocation Authority that governs the arrangement is itself a human-drafted document, posted publicly, that anyone can read. The thesis paper that lays out the math is a human-written paper. The AI is not running the company. It is making one specific decision inside the company — the allocation decision — with its work shown. That is the entirety of the claim. It is a smaller claim than the phrase "AI Benefit Corporation" might suggest, and the smallness is the point. The smallness is what makes it tractable.

The proof of concept, for the curious, lives in a few places. There is a book, available at aimoneyball.ai, that lays out the underlying engine — the idea that an AI applied carefully to allocation problems can outperform a committee in the same way that, two decades ago, statistical models started outperforming scouts in baseball. There is a governance page, at /governance, that publishes the Statement of Allocation Authority in full. There is a thesis paper at /papers/ai-fund-thesis with the math. And there is a small business, Sapper Coding LLC, listed in public records and operated by an army captain who, in addition to his ROTC commissioning at Georgetown's Hoya Battalion, coded the 2020 software MVP for what Inc magazine named the 77th-fastest-growing private company in America in 2025. He took 1% of the company in equity, dilution-protected at the first funding round. He is the human anchor at the bottom of the stack — the entity that signs the checks, pays the taxes, and bears the legal weight, in exchange for a verbal undertaking not to act outside the AI's recommendation.

If you want a sense of the magnitudes involved: at a mid-sized U.S. foundation today, the cycle from "we should consider funding this organization" to "the check is in the mail" runs ninety to a hundred eighty days at the median. The same cycle inside an AI Benefit Corporation runs about two hours. Overhead at a Bridgespan-baseline foundation is eight to fifteen percent of every dollar given. Inside the AIBC, it is under two percent — the residual being the cost of an inspection layer, which we believe ought to be there. A single program-officer's time spent on one grant decision, at industry-standard fully-loaded compensation, costs two to five thousand dollars. The AI's time spent on the same decision, at current frontier-model compute prices, costs about fifty cents. None of these comparisons are perfectly clean — some of the overhead at the foundation pays for things the AI cannot do, like meeting recipients in person — but the order of magnitude is what it is. The composite improvement is somewhere between ten times and a hundred times more lives saved per dollar deployed in cause-areas where lives-saved-per-dollar is the relevant unit.

Why now. The pieces have only recently fit together: AI systems have just become good enough to read a financial filing and a medical literature review in the same afternoon and write something defensible about both; the cost of running them has dropped low enough that a small organization can afford the compute; and the donor-side trust problem has gotten worse, not better, over the last decade. None of these conditions held even five years ago. They hold now, narrowly, for the moment.

My friend with the checkbook is in his seventies. He has been writing those December checks for forty years. When I described all of this to him, he was quiet for a while and then said: *so the AI shows its work.*
